Integration as the Reverse of Differentiation

$$\frac{d}{dx}\big[f(x)\big]=f'(x)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad\int f'(x)\,dx=f(x)+C$$
  • $f'(x)$ is the derivative of $f(x)$;   $f(x)$ is the anti-derivative of $f'(x)$
  • $C$ = arbitrary constant of integration — always include for indefinite integrals
  • $\displaystyle\int\cdots dx$ = "indefinite integral of … with respect to $x$"

Basic Properties

PropertyFormula
Addition / Subtraction$\displaystyle\int\big[f(x)\pm g(x)\big]dx=\int f(x)\,dx\pm\int g(x)\,dx$
Scalar Multiple$\displaystyle\int kf(x)\,dx=k\int f(x)\,dx,\quad k\in\mathbb{R},\,k\neq 0$
⚠Cannot split products or quotients: $\int x\sin x\,dx\neq\!\left(\int x\,dx\right)\!\!\left(\int\sin x\,dx\right)$. Also $\int xe^x\,dx\neq x\int e^x\,dx$.

5

Integration by Substitution

ℹThe substitution will always be given in exam questions when this method is required.

Procedure

  1. Let $u=g(x)$. Differentiate: $\dfrac{du}{dx}$. Rearrange for $dx$ in terms of $du$.
  2. Express all remaining $x$-terms using $u$ (using $x=g^{-1}(u)$ if needed).
  3. Integrate with respect to $u$.
  4. Substitute back: replace $u$ with $g(x)$ to express the answer in $x$.
Worked Example — Algebraic substitution

Use $u=1+2x$ to find $\displaystyle\int x(1+2x)^{11}\,dx$.

  • $u=1+2x\Rightarrow x=\dfrac{u-1}{2}$,   $\dfrac{du}{dx}=2\Rightarrow dx=\dfrac{du}{2}$
  • $=\displaystyle\int\dfrac{u-1}{2}\cdot u^{11}\cdot\dfrac{1}{2}\,du=\dfrac{1}{4}\int(u^{12}-u^{11})\,du=\dfrac{1}{4}\!\left(\dfrac{u^{13}}{13}-\dfrac{u^{12}}{12}\right)+C$
  • $=\dfrac{(1+2x)^{13}}{52}-\dfrac{(1+2x)^{12}}{48}+C$

Worked Example — Trig substitution

Use $x=3\cos\theta$ to find $\displaystyle\int\sqrt{9-x^2}\,dx$.

  • $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta}=-3\sin\theta$,   $9-x^2=9\sin^2\theta$, so $\sqrt{9-x^2}=3\sin\theta$ (since $\sin\theta\geq0$)
  • $=\displaystyle\int 3\sin\theta\cdot(-3\sin\theta)\,d\theta=-9\int\sin^2\theta\,d\theta=-\dfrac{9}{2}\!\left(\theta-\dfrac{\sin2\theta}{2}\right)+C$
  • Triangle from $x=3\cos\theta$: $\theta=\cos^{-1}\!\!\left(\dfrac{x}{3}\right)$, $\sin\theta=\dfrac{\sqrt{9-x^2}}{3}$, $\sin2\theta=\dfrac{2x\sqrt{9-x^2}}{9}$
  • $=-\dfrac{1}{2}\!\left[9\cos^{-1}\!\!\left(\dfrac{x}{3}\right)-x\sqrt{9-x^2}\right]+C$

Worked Example — $\displaystyle\int e^x\sin2x\,dx$
  • R1: $u=\sin2x$ (T), $\frac{dv}{dx}=e^x$ → $=e^x\sin2x-2\displaystyle\int e^x\cos2x\,dx$
  • R2 on $\displaystyle\int e^x\cos2x\,dx$: $u=\cos2x$ (T), $\frac{dv}{dx}=e^x$ → $=e^x\cos2x+2\displaystyle\int e^x\sin2x\,dx$
  • Substitute R2 into R1: $\displaystyle\int e^x\sin2x\,dx=e^x\sin2x-2e^x\cos2x-4\displaystyle\int e^x\sin2x\,dx$
  • $\Rightarrow 5\displaystyle\int e^x\sin2x\,dx=e^x(\sin2x-2\cos2x)+D$
  • $\Rightarrow\displaystyle\int e^x\sin2x\,dx=\dfrac{1}{5}e^x(\sin2x-2\cos2x)+C$ ✓
⚠Cyclic trap: Do NOT change your choice of $u$ between R1 and R2. Both rounds must use the same LIATE type as $u$ (T in the example above). Switching causes $0=0$ and no result.

Definite Integrals

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=\big[F(x)\big]_a^b=F(b)-F(a),\qquad F'(x)=f(x)$$
ℹ$C$ cancels in definite integrals — do not write it in working.

Definite Integrals with Substitution

  1. Change limits: substitute $x=a$ and $x=b$ into $u=g(x)$ → new limits $u_1,u_2$.
  2. Replace $dx$, integrand, and limits — all in terms of $u$.
  3. Integrate in $u$ with the new limits. No need to revert to $x$.
Worked Example

Definite Integral as a Limit of Sum

The key insight: slicing a region into infinitely thin rectangular strips and summing their areas in the limit gives an exact area, expressed as a definite integral.

Conceptual Development

Consider the area bounded by $y=f(x)$, the x-axis, $x=a$ and $x=b$. Divide $[a,b]$ into strips of width $\delta x$. Each strip has approximate area $\delta A \approx y\cdot\delta x$.

$$A=\lim_{\delta x\to 0}\sum_{x=a}^{b}y(\delta x)=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$
ℹThe thinner the strips ($\delta x\to 0$), the better the approximation — in the limit, the sum of rectangle areas equals the exact area. This is why the definite integral is defined as a limit of a sum.

Volume Analogue

The same idea extends to volumes: slice the solid into thin circular discs of radius $y$ and thickness $\delta x$. Each disc has volume $\approx \pi y^2 \delta x$, giving:

$$V=\lim_{\delta x\to 0}\sum_{x=a}^{b}\pi y^2(\delta x)=\pi\int_a^b y^2\,dx$$

Limit-of-Sum Problems (Exam Type)

These questions ask you to recognise a Riemann sum as a definite integral and evaluate it.

🧩 Reverse Detection Mode — "I don't see $\frac{1}{n}$ — what do I do?"

Examiners often present sums that don't immediately look like $\frac{1}{n}\sum f(\frac{r}{n})$. The fix: force it into that form by factoring.

$$\sum_{r=1}^{n}\frac{r}{n^2+r^2}=\sum_{r=1}^{n}\frac{r/n^2}{1+(r/n)^2}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n}\frac{r/n}{1+(r/n)^2}\;\xrightarrow{n\to\infty}\;\int_0^1\frac{x}{1+x^2}\,dx$$
  1. Look for $r/n$ in the expression — this will become $x$.
  2. Factor out $\frac{1}{n}$ to isolate the strip width.
  3. Identify $f(r/n)$ — replace every $r/n$ with $x$ to get $f(x)$.
  4. Write the limit as $\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx$ and evaluate.
Step 1 — Identify the structure
Write the sum in the form $\displaystyle\frac{1}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n}f\!\left(\frac{r}{n}\right)$ where the width of each strip is $\frac{1}{n}$ and the height is $f\!\left(\frac{r}{n}\right)$.
↓
Step 2 — Map to integral
As $n\to\infty$, this sum $\to\displaystyle\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx$. (Width $\frac{1}{n}\to dx$, height at $\frac{r}{n}\to f(x)$ at $x=\frac{r}{n}$.)
↓
Step 3 — Evaluate the definite integral
Compute $\displaystyle\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx$ using standard techniques from Chapter 9.
Worked Example 1 — [N2001/I/18 part]

(i) Find the exact value of $\displaystyle\int_0^1\frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx$.

(ii) Show that the total area $A$ of $n$ rectangles of width $\frac{1}{n}$ drawn under $y=\frac{1}{1+x^2}$ for $0\le x\le 1$ is:

$$A=\frac{1}{n}\left\{\frac{1}{1+\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^{\!2}}+\frac{1}{1+\!\left(\frac{2}{n}\right)^{\!2}}+\cdots+\frac{1}{2}\right\}$$

State the limit of $A$ as $n\to\infty$.

Solution (i):

$$\int_0^1\frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx=\left[\tan^{-1}x\right]_0^1=\tan^{-1}1-\tan^{-1}0=\frac{\pi}{4}-0=\frac{\pi}{4}$$

Solution (ii): Width of each rectangle $=\frac{1}{n}$.

Height of $r$th rectangle $=f\!\left(\frac{r}{n}\right)=\dfrac{1}{1+\left(\frac{r}{n}\right)^2}$, for $r=1,2,\ldots,n$.

$$A=\frac{1}{n}\left(\frac{1}{1+\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^2}+\frac{1}{1+\left(\frac{2}{n}\right)^2}+\cdots+\frac{1}{1+\left(\frac{n}{n}\right)^2}\right)=\frac{1}{n}\left\{\frac{1}{1+\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^2}+\cdots+\frac{1}{2}\right\}\quad\text{(shown)}$$

As $n\to\infty$: $A\to\displaystyle\int_0^1\frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$.

ℹThe last term when $r=n$ gives $\frac{1}{1+(n/n)^2}=\frac{1}{1+1}=\frac{1}{2}$. As $n\to\infty$, the number of rectangles increases and the width of each decreases, so the sum approaches the exact area.
Worked Example — [N2015/I/Q3]

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\left(\frac{\sqrt[3]{1}+\sqrt[3]{2}+\cdots+\sqrt[3]{n}}{\sqrt[3]{n}}\right)$.

Solution: Rewrite as:

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n}\frac{\sqrt[3]{r}}{\sqrt[3]{n}}=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n}\left(\frac{r}{n}\right)^{1/3}=\int_0^1 x^{1/3}\,dx=\left[\frac{x^{4/3}}{4/3}\right]_0^1=\frac{3}{4}$$

Using Definite Integrals to Find Area

The fundamental process: sketch → identify sign → set up integral → evaluate. Never skip the sketch.

2.1 Area Bounded by a Curve and the x-axis

Region above the x-axis [$f(x) \ge 0$]

$$A=\int_a^b y\,dx=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$

Each vertical rectangular strip has area $y\cdot\delta x > 0$, so the integral is positive.

Region below the x-axis [$f(x) \le 0$]

$$A=-\int_a^b y\,dx=-\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$

The integral $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ is negative when $f(x)\le 0$ (each strip has height $-y>0$). The negative sign in front converts it to a positive area.

⚠Mixed regions (partly above, partly below x-axis): You MUST split the integral at the x-intercept(s). Do NOT try to compute a single integral over the entire range — you will get cancellation, not total area.
$$\text{Total area} = \int_a^c f(x)\,dx + \left(-\int_c^b f(x)\,dx\right)$$

where $c$ is the x-value where $f(c)=0$ between $a$ and $b$.

Worked Example 2

Find the exact area bounded by $y=x^2+1$, the lines $x=1$, $x=3$, and the x-axis.

Since $y=x^2+1>0$ for all $x$, the region is entirely above the x-axis.

$$A=\int_1^3(x^2+1)\,dx=\left[\frac{x^3}{3}+x\right]_1^3=\left(\frac{27}{3}+3\right)-\left(\frac{1}{3}+1\right)=10+3-\frac{1}{3}-1=\frac{32}{3}\text{ units}^2$$
Worked Example 3

Find the area of the region bounded by the x-axis and the curve $y=(x-1)(x-4)$.

The curve crosses the x-axis at $x=1$ and $x=4$. For $1\le x\le 4$, $(x-1)(x-4)\le 0$ (region is below x-axis).

$$A=-\int_1^4(x-1)(x-4)\,dx=-\int_1^4(x^2-5x+4)\,dx=-\left[\frac{x^3}{3}-\frac{5x^2}{2}+4x\right]_1^4=\frac{9}{2}\text{ units}^2$$
💡A GC will give $-4.5$ for $\int_1^4(x-1)(x-4)dx$. The area is $+4.5=\frac{9}{2}$ units². The GC value is the signed area; always take the absolute value for the geometric area when below the axis.

2.2 Area Bounded by a Curve and the y-axis

Instead of vertical strips (width $\delta x$), we use horizontal strips (height $\delta y$). Express $x$ as a function of $y$, then integrate with respect to $y$.

Region right of y-axis [$g(y) \ge 0$]

$$A=\int_p^q x\,dy=\int_p^q g(y)\,dy$$

Region left of y-axis [$g(y) \le 0$]

$$A=-\int_p^q x\,dy=-\int_p^q g(y)\,dy$$
ℹKey step: Rearrange the curve equation to get $x$ in terms of $y$ before integrating. The limits $p$ and $q$ are y-values, not x-values.
Worked Example 5

Find the area of the finite region enclosed by the parabola $y^2=4-x$ and the y-axis.

When $x=0$: $y=\pm 2$. Express $x$ in terms of $y$: $x=4-y^2$. Since $x\ge 0$ for the region between the curve and y-axis:

$$A=\int_{-2}^{2}x\,dy=\int_{-2}^{2}(4-y^2)\,dy=\left[4y-\frac{y^3}{3}\right]_{-2}^{2}=\left(8-\frac{8}{3}\right)-\left(-8+\frac{8}{3}\right)=\frac{32}{3}\text{ units}^2$$
Worked Example 6

Find the exact area of the region enclosed by $y=3-\dfrac{1}{x+1}$, the y-axis, and the lines $y=-1$ and $y=\dfrac{5}{2}$.

Rearrange: $x=\dfrac{1}{3-y}-1$. Note the curve has vertical asymptote at $x=-1$. The region is partly to the left ($x\le 0$ for $y\le 2$) and partly to the right ($x\ge 0$ for $2\le y\le\frac{5}{2}$) of the y-axis.

$$A=-\int_{-1}^{2}x\,dy+\int_{2}^{5/2}x\,dy=-\int_{-1}^{2}\!\!\left(\frac{1}{3-y}-1\right)dy+\int_{2}^{5/2}\!\!\left(\frac{1}{3-y}-1\right)dy$$
$$=\left[-\ln|3-y|-y\right]_{-1}^{2}\cdot(-1)+\left[-\ln|3-y|-y\right]_{2}^{5/2}\cdot(1)=\frac{5}{2}-\ln 2\text{ units}^2$$
⚠Always check whether the region crosses the y-axis (i.e. whether $x=g(y)$ changes sign within the interval). If it does, split the integral at the y-value where $g(y)=0$.

2.3 Area Bounded by a Curve and a Line

When a region is bounded by both a curve and a line, it is sometimes easier to integrate with respect to $y$ (horizontal strips). Split the region into sub-regions as needed.

Worked Example 7

The region $R$ in the first quadrant is bounded by $y=\dfrac{4}{x}$, the lines $y=2x$, $x=0$ and $y=4$. Find the exact area of $R$.

Find intersection of $y=\frac{4}{x}$ and $y=2x$: $\frac{4}{x}=2x\Rightarrow x^2=2\Rightarrow x=\sqrt{2}$ (since $x>0$), so $y=2\sqrt{2}$.

Split $R$ into $R_1$ (bounded above by $y=4$, right by $y=\frac{4}{x}$) and $R_2$ (triangle bounded by $y=2x$, $y=2\sqrt{2}$, and x-axis region). Integrate w.r.t. $y$:

$$A=\int_{2\sqrt{2}}^{4}x\,dy+\text{Area of triangle}=\int_{2\sqrt{2}}^{4}\frac{4}{y}\,dy+\frac{1}{2}(\sqrt{2})(2\sqrt{2})$$
$$=\left[4\ln|y|\right]_{2\sqrt{2}}^{4}+2=4(\ln 4-\ln 2\sqrt{2})+2=4\ln\!\left(\frac{4}{2\sqrt{2}}\right)+2=4\ln\sqrt{2}+2=2\ln 2+2\text{ units}^2$$

2.4 Area Bounded by Two Curves

2.4.1 — Two curves $y = f(x)$ and $y = g(x)$

In all three cases (region above x-axis, below x-axis, crossing x-axis), the formula is the same:

$$A=\int_a^b\bigl(f(x)-g(x)\bigr)\,dx\qquad\text{where }f(x)\ge g(x)\text{ on }[a,b]$$

Think of the integrand as (upper curve) − (lower curve). This works regardless of whether the region is above or below the x-axis.

ℹTo find $a$ and $b$: set $f(x)=g(x)$ and solve for the x-coordinates of the intersection points.
Worked Example 8

Find the area bounded by $y=(2x+1)(x+3)$ and $y=e^{x/2}$.

2.5 Definite Integrals Involving Modulus Sign

When the integrand contains $|f(x)|$, you cannot integrate directly. You must remove the modulus by splitting the range of integration.

Step 1
Find where $f(x)=0$ in the interval $[a,b]$. These are the split points.
↓
Step 2
Determine the sign of $f(x)$ on each sub-interval (sketch the curve $y=f(x)$ or solve inequality).
↓
Step 3 — Replace the modulus
On sub-intervals where $f(x)\ge 0$: $|f(x)|=f(x)$. On sub-intervals where $f(x)<0$: $|f(x)|=-f(x)$.
↓
Step 4
Integrate each piece separately and add the results.
⚠This is a "without GC" technique. Questions that say "without a GC" or "exact value" involving $|f(x)|$ require this method. GC gives a numerical answer but not the exact form.

Volume of Solid of Revolution

When a region is rotated about a straight line, the solid formed is called a solid of revolution. The volume is found by summing the volumes of thin circular discs.

ℹDisc method intuition: A thin vertical strip of width $\delta x$ and height $y$, when rotated 360° about the x-axis, sweeps out a disc of radius $y$ and thickness $\delta x$. Volume of disc $\approx \pi y^2\delta x$. Summing all discs and taking the limit gives the integral.
📏 Hard Law — Axis Dictates Variable (H2 Syllabus Boundary)
$y = k$ (horizontal)
→ Use $dx$ (vertical strips)
$V=\pi\int_a^b(\text{radius})^2\,dx$
$x = h$ (vertical)
→ Use $dy$ (horizontal strips)
$V=\pi\int_p^q(\text{radius})^2\,dy$
⚠Using the other variable requires the Cylindrical Shell Method — which is NOT in the H2 syllabus. Attempting it will produce wrong or impossible algebra. If you find yourself needing it, you are almost certainly misidentifying the axis.

3.1 Volume of Revolution about the x-axis

$$V=\pi\int_a^b y^2\,dx=\pi\int_a^b[f(x)]^2\,dx$$

The region is bounded by $y=f(x)$, the x-axis, and the lines $x=a$, $x=b$. Rotated completely (360°) about the x-axis.

💡"Four right angles" = 360° = $2\pi$ radians = "completely" = one full revolution. These are all equivalent.
Worked Example 12

Region $R$ in the first quadrant is bounded by $y=2x^{1/3}$, the x-axis and $x=8$. Find the exact volume when $R$ is rotated through four right angles about the x-axis.

$$V=\pi\int_0^8 y^2\,dx=\pi\int_0^8\left(2x^{1/3}\right)^2dx=\pi\int_0^8 4x^{2/3}\,dx=4\pi\left[\frac{x^{5/3}}{5/3}\right]_0^8=\frac{12\pi}{5}\left[x^{5/3}\right]_0^8=\frac{12\pi}{5}(32)=\frac{384\pi}{5}\text{ units}^3$$
Try This!

Region $R$ is bounded by $y=(4x^2+9a^2)^{-1/2}$, the x-axis, the y-axis and $x=1.5a$ ($a>0$). Find the volume when $R$ is rotated completely about the x-axis.

$$V=\pi\int_0^{3a/2}\frac{1}{4x^2+9a^2}\,dx=\frac{\pi}{9a^2}\int_0^{3a/2}\frac{1}{\frac{4x^2}{9a^2}+1}\,dx$$

Let $u=\frac{2x}{3a}$, matching $\frac{1}{a}\tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{x}{a}\right)$ form with $a_0=\frac{3a}{2}$:

$$V=\frac{\pi}{4a}\left[\frac{1}{3}\tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{2x}{3a}\right)\right]_0^{3a/2}=\frac{\pi}{12a}\cdot\frac{\pi}{4}=\frac{\pi^2}{48a}\text{ units}^3$$

3.2 Volume of Revolution about the y-axis

$$V=\pi\int_p^q x^2\,dy=\pi\int_p^q[g(y)]^2\,dy$$

The region is bounded by $x=g(y)$, the y-axis, and the lines $y=p$, $y=q$. Rotated completely about the y-axis. Express $x^2$ in terms of $y$ before integrating.

Worked Example 13

The region bounded by $y=e^{x+1}$, $y=1$ and the y-axis is rotated through 360° about the y-axis. Find the numerical value of the volume.

Express $x$ in terms of $y$: $y=e^{x+1}\Rightarrow x=\ln y-1$. Limits: from $y=1$ (when $x=0$) to $y=e$ (when $x=1$).

$$V=\pi\int_1^e x^2\,dy=\pi\int_1^e(\ln y-1)^2\,dy\approx 1.37\text{ units}^3$$
💡"Numerical value" is a hint you may use GC to evaluate the integral directly.
Worked Example — Trig Substitution in Volume

Region $Q$ is bounded by $y=\dfrac{x}{\sqrt[3]{1-4x^2}}$, $x=-\frac{1}{4}$, $x=\frac{1}{4}$, and the x-axis. Using the substitution $2x=\sin\theta$, find the exact volume when $Q$ is rotated 4 right angles about the x-axis.

By symmetry, the region from $x=-\frac{1}{4}$ to $x=0$ is the mirror of $x=0$ to $x=\frac{1}{4}$:

$$V=\pi\int_{-1/4}^{1/4}y^2\,dx=2\pi\int_0^{1/4}\frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1-4x^2}}\,dx$$

Substitution: $2x=\sin\theta$, $x=\frac{1}{2}\sin\theta$, $dx=\frac{1}{2}\cos\theta\,d\theta$. When $x=0$: $\theta=0$; when $x=\frac{1}{4}$: $\theta=\frac{\pi}{6}$.

$$=2\pi\int_0^{\pi/6}\frac{\frac{1}{4}\sin^2\theta}{\sqrt{1-\sin^2\theta}}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cos\theta\,d\theta=\frac{\pi}{4}\int_0^{\pi/6}\sin^2\theta\,d\theta=\frac{\pi}{8}\int_0^{\pi/6}(1-\cos 2\theta)\,d\theta$$
$$=\frac{\pi}{8}\left[\theta-\frac{1}{2}\sin 2\theta\right]_0^{\pi/6}=\frac{\pi}{8}\left[\frac{\pi}{6}-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\right]=\frac{\pi}{96}(2\pi-3\sqrt{3})\text{ units}^3$$

3.3 Volume for Region Bounded by Two Curves

When the region is bounded by two curves, the solid of revolution has a "hole" in the middle (like a washer). Use subtraction:

$$V_{\text{total}}=V_{\text{larger solid}}-V_{\text{smaller solid}}$$
About x-axis
$V=\pi\int_a^b y_{\text{upper}}^2\,dx - \pi\int_a^b y_{\text{lower}}^2\,dx$
About y-axis
$V=\pi\int_p^q x_{\text{right}}^2\,dy - \pi\int_p^q x_{\text{left}}^2\,dy$
🚨 #1 Algebraic Error — The Washer Square Trap
✗ Wrong — Do NOT do this
$$V=\pi\int_a^b\bigl(y_{\text{upper}}-y_{\text{lower}}\bigr)^2\,dx$$

Expanding $(y_1-y_2)^2=y_1^2-2y_1y_2+y_2^2$ gives extra cross-term $-2y_1y_2$ which is physically meaningless.

✓ Right — Square each radius first, THEN subtract
$$V=\pi\int_a^b\bigl(y_{\text{upper}}^2-y_{\text{lower}}^2\bigr)\,dx$$
🧠Memory hook: "Square → then subtract. NOT subtract → then square." Each radius is squared independently because the disc area formula is $\pi r^2$ applied to each radius separately.
Worked Example 15

Region $R$ is bounded by $y^2=x$ and $y=x$. Find the exact volume when $R$ is rotated one revolution about the x-axis.

Intersections: $y^2=x$ and $y=x\Rightarrow x^2=x\Rightarrow x=0$ or $x=1$. On $[0,1]$: $y=\sqrt{x}$ is upper, $y=x$ is lower (in the first quadrant).

Larger solid: rotate region under $y=\sqrt{x}$ (equivalently, $y^2=x$) about x-axis.

Smaller solid: rotate triangle (under $y=x$) about x-axis — this is a cone with radius 1, height 1.

$$V=\pi\int_0^1 x\,dx-\frac{1}{3}\pi(1)^2(1)=\pi\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^1-\frac{\pi}{3}=\frac{\pi}{2}-\frac{\pi}{3}=\frac{\pi}{6}\text{ units}^3$$

3.4 Composite Solids

A composite solid is formed when different parts of the region are bounded by different curves. The total volume is the sum of the constituent volumes.

$$V_{\text{composite}}=V_1+V_2+\cdots$$
ℹCommon composite: one piece bounded by a curve up to some y-value, plus a cylinder for the rest. Always draw the picture to see which pieces form the solid.

3.5 Rotation about an Axis Parallel to x-axis or y-axis

When the axis is $y=k$ (not the x-axis), or $x=h$ (not the y-axis), you cannot apply the standard formulas directly. You must translate the region first.

Strategy — Translation Method
The volume of revolution formed when rotating $R$ about $y=k$ is the same as the volume formed when rotating the translated region $S$ about the x-axis, where $S$ is obtained by translating $R$ downward by $k$ units (i.e. replace $y$ with $y-k$).
↓
In practice
If curve $C$ has equation $y=f(x)$ and axis is $y=k$, the translated curve $C'$ has equation $y=f(x)-k$. Apply $V=\pi\int_a^b(y-k)^2\,dx$ — or equivalently, use the new $y$ in the standard formula.
🎯 Distance Thinking — The Sign-Proof Mental Model

Instead of memorising whether to write $(f(x)-k)$ or $(k-f(x))$, think geometrically:

$$V=\pi\int_a^b(\text{radius})^2\,dx \qquad\text{where radius}=\text{distance from curve to rotation axis}$$
If curve is above axis $y=k$
Radius $= f(x)-k \ge 0$. Write $(f(x)-k)^2$.
If curve is below axis $y=k$
Radius $= k-f(x) \ge 0$. Write $(k-f(x))^2$.
💡Key insight: $(f(x)-k)^2=(k-f(x))^2$ always — squaring eliminates the sign. So whether you write $f(x)-k$ or $k-f(x)$, the volume formula gives the same answer. Just use whichever makes the non-negative nature obvious.
⚠The translated region $S$ (bounded by new curve $C'$ and x-axis) has the same shape and size as region $R$ (bounded by $C$ and $y=k$). So their volumes of revolution about the respective axes are equal.

Common Mistakes — Wrong vs Right

The most penalised errors in A-Level examinations. Study each one carefully.

✗ Wrong — Area below x-axis$\displaystyle\int_1^4(x-1)(x-4)\,dx = \text{area}$
✓ RightArea $=-\displaystyle\int_1^4(x-1)(x-4)\,dx$ since region is below x-axis. GC gives $-4.5$; area $=\frac{9}{2}$.
✗ Wrong — Mixed-sign single integral$\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ for region crossing x-axis at $c\in(a,b)$
✓ RightSplit: $\displaystyle\int_a^c f(x)\,dx+\left(-\int_c^b f(x)\,dx\right)$. Never integrate across a sign change for area.
✗ Wrong — Forgetting to make x the subject$\displaystyle\int_p^q y\,dy$ when computing area w.r.t. y-axis
✓ RightExpress $x=g(y)$, then $\displaystyle\int_p^q g(y)\,dy$. Limits must be y-values; integrand must be $x$ as a function of $y$.
✗ Wrong — Volume without $\pi$$V=\displaystyle\int_a^b y^2\,dx$
✓ Right$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b y^2\,dx$. Always include $\pi$ — each disc has cross-sectional area $\pi r^2=\pi y^2$.
✗ Wrong — Not squaring before integrating$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b y\,dx$ (forgot to square $y$)
✓ Right$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b y^2\,dx$. The disc has radius $y$, so area $=\pi y^2$ (not $\pi y$).
✗ Wrong — Two-curve volume without subtraction$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b y_1^2\,dx+\pi\int_a^b y_2^2\,dx$
✓ Right$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b y_1^2\,dx-\pi\int_a^b y_2^2\,dx$ (larger minus smaller). Adding gives wrong solid.
✗ Wrong — Parallel axis without translationRotation about $y=2$: use $V=\pi\int y^2\,dx$ directly
✓ RightReplace $y$ with $y-2$: $V=\pi\int(y-2)^2\,dx$. Alternatively, translate the curve down by 2 and rotate about x-axis.
✗ Wrong — Modulus integral without splitting$\displaystyle\int_0^{\sqrt{2}}|x^2+2x-3|\,dx=\left[\frac{x^3}{3}+x^2-3x\right]_0^{\sqrt{2}}$
✓ RightFind zeros in $[0,\sqrt{2}]$: $x=1$. Split at $x=1$. Negate integrand on $[0,1]$ (negative there), keep positive on $[1,\sqrt{2}]$.
✗ Wrong — Not sketching firstSetting up integral without knowing sign/position of region
✓ RightAlways sketch the curve(s), mark the bounded region, and identify: above/below axis, intersection points, limits of integration.
✗ Wrong — Limit-of-sum: wrong limits$\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n}f\!\!\left(\frac{r}{n}\right)\to\int_1^2 f(x)\,dx$
✓ RightWhen the sum starts at $r=1$ with height $f(r/n)$ and width $1/n$, the limit is $\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx$ (not $\int_1^2$). The variable goes from $0$ to $1$.
✗ Wrong — Washer: subtract then square$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b(y_{\text{upper}}-y_{\text{lower}})^2\,dx$
✓ Right — Square each, then subtract$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b(y_{\text{upper}}^2-y_{\text{lower}}^2)\,dx$. Cross-term from wrong form is physically meaningless.
✗ Wrong — Axis shift: sign confusionCurve $y=f(x)$ below axis $y=k$: unsure whether $(f(x)-k)$ or $(k-f(x))$

✗ Wrong — Wrong variable for rotation axisRotation about $y=3$ (horizontal): integrating w.r.t. $dy$ instead of $dx$
✓ Right — Axis dictates variableHorizontal axis ($y=k$) → $dx$. Vertical axis ($x=h$) → $dy$. Other direction requires Shell Method (NOT in H2 syllabus).
✗ Wrong — Limit of sum: $\frac{1}{n}$ not visibleTreating $\sum\frac{r}{n^2+r^2}$ as though it cannot be converted to an integral

Exam Tips & Special Scenarios

When the Question Says "Exact"

⚠The word "exact" means you must show algebraic working to evaluate the definite integral. Do NOT use GC to evaluate integrals directly. You may still use GC to check your answer, but the working must be shown analytically.

When the Question Says "Numerical Value"

✓The phrase "numerical value" is a hint that you MAY (and should) use GC to evaluate the definite integral. Give a decimal answer. This typically appears in volume problems where analytic integration would be very difficult.

GC Usage for Intersection Points

  • Always give intersection x-coordinates to at least 5 significant figures when using GC as intermediate values.
  • State the values clearly before setting up the integral: "From GC, $x\approx -3.042941$ or $x\approx -0.3414448$".
  • For exact-form questions, find intersections algebraically.

Symmetry Shortcut

When the integrand or region is symmetric, exploit it:

  • If curve is symmetric about y-axis: $\int_{-a}^{a}f(x)\,dx=2\int_0^a f(x)\,dx$ (when $f$ is even).
  • If region is symmetric about x-axis: integrate the upper half and double.
  • In volume problems: if the volume element is symmetric about a vertical axis, integrate one half and multiply by 2.
